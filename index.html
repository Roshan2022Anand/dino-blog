Dinosaur (clade Dinosauria)

the common name given to a group of reptiles, often very large, that first appeared roughly 245 million years ago (near the beginning of the Middle Triassic Epoch) and thrived worldwide for nearly 180 million years.

SHOW MORE...

The name dinosaur comes from the Greek words deinos (“terrible” or “fearfully great”) and sauros (“reptile” or “lizard”). The English anatomist Richard Owen proposed the formal term Dinosauria in 1842 to include three giant extinct animals (Megalosaurus, Iguanodon, and Hylaeosaurus) represented by large fossilized bones that had been unearthed at several locations in southern England during the early part of the 19th century.

Before 1818
The first finds

Before Richard Owen introduced the term Dinosauria in 1842, there was no concept of anything even like a dinosaur. Large fossilized bones quite probably had been observed long before that time, but there is little record—and no existing specimens—of such findings much before 1818. In any case, people could not have been expected to understand what dinosaurs were even if they found their remains.

SHOW MORE...

In 1676 Robert Plot of the University of Oxford included, in a work of natural history, a drawing of what was apparently the knee-end of the thighbone of a dinosaur, which he thought might have come from an elephant taken to Britain in Roman times.

In 1850s
Reconstruction and classification

During the decades that followed Owen’s announcement, many other kinds of dinosaurs were discovered and named in England and Europe: Massospondylus in 1854, Scelidosaurus in 1859, Bothriospondylus in 1875, and Omosaurus in 1877.

SHOW MORE...

Popular fascination with the giant reptiles grew, reaching a peak in the 1850s with the first attempts to reconstruct the three animals on which Owen based Dinosauria—Iguanodon, Megalosaurus, and Hylaeosaurus—for the first world exposition, the Great Exhibition of 1851 in London’s Crystal Palace.

In 1860s
Dinosaur ancestors

During the early decades of dinosaur discoveries, little thought was given to their evolutionary ancestry. Not only were the few specimens known unlike any living animal, but they were so different from any other reptiles that it was difficult to discern much about their relationships.

SHOW MORE...

Early on it was recognized that, as a group, dinosaurs appear to be most closely allied to crocodilians, though T.H. Huxley had proposed in the 1860s that dinosaurs and birds must have had a very close common ancestor in the distant past.

PRESENT
Modern studies

During the first century or more of dinosaur awareness, workers in the field more or less concentrated on the search for new specimens and new types. Their discoveries then required detailed description and analysis, followed by comparisons with other known dinosaurs in order to classify the new finds and develop hypotheses about evolutionary relationships.

SHOW MORE...

These pursuits continue, but newer methods of exploration and analysis have been adopted. Emphasis has shifted from purely descriptive procedures to analyses of relationships by using the methods of cladistics, which dispenses with the traditional taxonomic hierarchy in favour of “phylogenetic trees” that are more explicit about evolutionary relationships.

Tyrannosaurus rex
Late Cretaceous (about 68 to 66 million years ago)
VIEW INFO

The Tyrannosaurus rex, often referred to as T. rex, is one of the most well-known dinosaurs. It was a large carnivorous dinosaur that lived during the late Cretaceous period. It had powerful jaws and teeth, capable of crushing bone.

Triceratops
Late Cretaceous (about 68 to 66 million years ago)
VIEW INFO

Triceratops is a genus of herbivorous ceratopsid dinosaur that first appeared during the late Maastrichtian stage of the late Cretaceous period. It is known for its three horns and large bony frill.

Velociraptor
Late Cretaceous (about 75 to 71 million years ago)
VIEW INFO

Velociraptor was a small but agile and predatory dinosaur. It had a long, sickle-shaped claw on each hind foot, which it likely used to hunt its prey. It lived during the late Cretaceous period.

Stegosaurus
Late Jurassic (about 155 to 150 million years ago)
VIEW INFO

Stegosaurus is one of the most recognizable dinosaurs, known for its distinctive row of large bony plates along its back and the spikes on its tail. It was a herbivore that lived during the late Jurassic period.
